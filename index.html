Personal Data in Galleries PhD Project

Hello there! My name is Harriet Cameron and I'm a PhD student in the Horizon CDT, based at the Mixed Reality Lab, University of Nottingham. My PhD is all about personal data, power, and privacy, and aims to create an ethical, transparent, and reciprocal method with art galleries to collect personal data.

On this site you will find more information about the study, as well as links to resources if you're interested in learning more about personal data privacy, exploitation, and potential futures.

What is personal data?

Personal data is defined by the European Commission as "…any information that relates to an identified or identifiable living individual." This also includes "different pieces of information, which collected together can lead to the identification of a particular person". This means that the term 'personal data' even includes information that might seem anonymised on its own, for example age, occupation, gender, sexuality, health status, hair colour... Even things like your fingerprints.

Personal Data

Personal data is collected about us every single day, from dozens of different sources. Your social media accounts, the news sites you read, the public wifi you connect to, CCTV cameras you pass, so on and so on and so on. This data can be used for good, or for bad, depending on who is collecting and why.

Power

That personal data has power. But so do you! Sometimes, data is used in ways that are hidden or deceptive, preventing the power from being shared equally. By shedding light on these practices, it becomes possible to redistribute power to make sure you have control over what happens to your data.

Why Art Galleries?

Art galleries are uniquely situated at the forefront of culture to share information to a huge number of people. They are able to disseminate and spread information about your rights as data subjects, and to encourage healthier and more beneficial practices surrounding the use of your personal data.

A little more detail...

I want to make sure that you have the power over your own data to make informed decisions and protect yourselves

Data exploitation is an increasingly problematic source of concern and affects each and every one of us. From how much your mortgage costs, to the adverts you see, the recommendations suggested to you; each of these things can positively affect your lives, but they can also be dangerous - particularly if you are part of a marginalised community.

How does data exploitation affect you, and what can you do?

Data is a commodity, it’s worth something. By donating your data to organisations you pay for services like social media, news, and information sites. Your data helps companies understand you better, and this helps them evolve and grow and change to better meet demand. It makes googling something easier, your recommendations more accurate, it even makes your adverts relevant and useful to you.

But data also has a sinister side, one that is at least as prevelant as the positive side. Profiling based on personal data can be used to prevent people from accessing specific resources. For example, it has been used to profile people searching for a new house and decide which houses they can see based on factors like race, religion, sex, or disability. It can also be used to target people based on their vulnerabilities. In 2019 a Casumo gambling advert was banned for targeting people who

searched on Google for "how to unsubscribe from all gambling", deliberately targeting people with a gambling addiction to try to get them to play casino games. There are thousands and thousands of examples of how personal data has been used to exploit, hurt, or discriminate against people. Airlines increasing flight costs for repeat searches; online quizzes harvesting data and infecting computers with viruses; algorithms unearthing private information and unwittingly sharing it with people's friends and family; and of course the Cambridge Analytica scandal interfering with political campaigning.

However, data is a vital commodity that can be used mindfully and ethically to genuinely improve experiences, and one which is particularly vital for the cultural sector. For much of the UK's cultural sector, they rely on external funding (like the Arts Council, local councils, or sponsors) to survive. Those funding partners usually require the galleries to collect certain kinds of data to showcase how their money is being used. Data also helps organisations like galleries to understand their role in the world, how

they are affecting their communities, and how they can continue to evolve to maintain relevance and make an impact. So if data collection is so widely used for both good and for bad, how do you protect yourselves from exploitation whilst still contributing to those who will genuinely benefit from and protect your data? The first thing is simply to be aware of what data you are donating, what is happening to that data, and what it's being used for. When you start to pay attention to the practices in place, it quickly becomes easier to understand and make informed decisions about who you want to have your data, and which parts you're willing to share.

The key is to learn, to talk to your friends and family, and to be mindful of the value of your data and the power you are giving away or sharing every time you donate it. In the next section I'll guide you to some resources that will offer you some starting points for this.

Resources and links

So how can you learn more?

It can be pretty overwhelming at first to start to look into something like personal data collection and that's ok. In some cases, it might even be deliberately confusing. But there are hundreds of resources out there that can show you what to look for, and easy steps you can take to protect yourselves.

A starting point

To the right of this text are links to six different resource. I've tried to include a range of different types of resources so that if one of them isn't your cup of tea, a different one might still be useful!
1. The Electronic Frontier Foundation website. EFF is a not-for-profit organisation who work to keep people safe online.
2. A data themed YouTube video by PhilosophyTube who uses incredibly engaging formats to talk about contemporary issues.
3. A Netflix documentary about the Cambridge Analytica scandal (you will need access to a Netflix account to watch it).
4. A book by Caroline Criado-Perez that discusses and offers in depth examples of data collection (the link goes to bookseller Hive, who donate a portion of each book sold to a local bookshop of your choice).
5. A Cambridge University research project that will offer you an idea of what behind-the-scenes social media profiling thinks of you. Withdrawing your details from the study is also easy, should you choose to do so.
6. WIRED - an online news source which covers a huge spectrum of tech related news articles.
If you have any other resources that you've really enjoyed or think should be on this list, do feel free to drop me an email and let me know!

About me and contact info

I am an interdisciplinary PhD researcher at the Mixed Reality Lab, University of Nottingham, based in the Horizon CDT.
